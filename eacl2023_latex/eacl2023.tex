\section{Introduction} \label{sec:intro}

Digitization of historical texts has opened up new opportunities for 
researchers to explore a large-scale corpus of historical texts
with computational methods. Especially, the ready availability
of datasets required for Natural Language Processing (NLP) research 
has welcomed researchers from other disciplines to NLP research \cite{park2022raison} 
and reduced the barriers to entry of NLP research.
Taking this advantage, many researchers in diverse field,
namely Sociology, History, English, and Information Science 
have applied NLP techniques to historical texts, such as books \cite{parulian2022uncovering}, 
newspapers \cite{smith2013infectious,pedrazzini2022machines,santin2016or},
and/or congressional records \cite{lin2022enhancing, guldi2019parliament}.
Creating an interdisciplinary research space called \textit{Digital Humanities}, 
there have been studies primarily focusing on the race problem in the United States.
To introduce a few large-scale computational research, 
\citet{soni2021abolitionist} traced the semantic change of the word, for instance
when and which newspaper started to use the word with a new meaning and when and which
newspapers adopted new semantic meaning of the word in abolitionist newspapers from the 19th century. 
\citet{franzosi2012ways} analyzed racial violence, especially lynching performed by the White mob
in the 19th century Georgia and presented quantitative narratives of the racial violence.
These studies intersect the problem of historical racism and NLP research.
However, despite the increasing attention toward the large scale text analysis on 
historical racism, there has been a gap of understanding the racism posed toward Asians in the United States.
While it is true that slavery of African Americans and abolition of slavery 
has led to secession of the Southern states and the Civil War, Asian workers who
are immigrants from China and India also created a racial tension in the United States.
Asian immigrant workers were called \textit{``coolies''}.
Britannica entry on \textit{``coolie''} introduces the word as ``pejorative European usage'' to 
describe ``an unskilled labourer or porter usually in or from the Far East hired for low
or subsistence wages.'' \footnote{\url{https://www.britannica.com/money/topic/coolie-Asian-labourer}}
In seeking to fill the labor shortage in the United States due to the
abolition, Chinese workers were recruited and migration of Chinese workers arrived in the United States,
beyond China-neighboring countries like India and Malaysia \cite{farley1968chinese}.

\subsection{Application of NLP to Historical Texts} \label{sec:application_nlp}
Applying NLP to the historical text is not a new idea. 
\citet{pedrazzini2022machines} used a diachronic word embedding
to trace the semantic change of the word associated with Industrial
Revolution and presented how the meaning of the word shifted 
in digitized 19th century British newspapers. 
\citet{soni2021abolitionist} also used the abolitionist newspapers from
the 19th century to trace semantic changes of the word by using 
diachronic word embeddings similar to \citeauthor{pedrazzini2022machines}.
Another research group took a large scale digitized historical newspapers
and used NLP technique to detect the text reprints in the historical newspapers \cite{smith2013infectious}

While aforementioned studies used historical newspapers as a primary corpus for the analysis,
there also have been studies on applying NLP to historical literature.
\citet{parulian2022uncovering} studied word features by comparing
Bag-of-Words and full-text embeddings and classified 
Black Fantastic genre with feature-based classifiers.
Another study using Project Gutenberg corpus compared 
deep learning models and traditional feature-based method for 
genre classification \cite{worsham2018genre}.

However, there has been a gap of understanding the White supremacist literature, 
especially the genre called \textit{``Anti-Tom''} and there has been no work on applying
datasets created in the 21st century to the literature written in the 20th century. 
This study attempts to fill this gap by applying the datasets created in the 21st century
and examine the performance of the classifiers. The advancement of hate speech detection
in NLP is presented in the following section \ref{sec:hate_speech_detection}.

\subsection{Hate Speech Detection} \label{sec:hate_speech_detection}
The rise of social media has made easy-to-access text data available for NLP research.
The online communication space play a role as a space for sharing
ideas and opinions \cite{ocal2021reasoning}, but its anonymity and lack of accountability 
have led to the proliferation of hate speech \cite{fortuna2020toxic}.
Social media platform like Twitter and Reddit has attracted researchers to 
collect, annotate, and publish datasets for hate speech detection \cite{waseem2016hateful,kennedy2017technology}. Among 
many other datasets, \citet{wulczyn2017ex} collected personal attack
comments from Wikipedia discussion pages and annotated them with crowd-sourcing. 
Another popular hate speech dataset is from Twitter with three 
categories of racism, sexism, and neither \cite{waseem2016hateful}. 

While these datasets have collected text data from online space that is not
necessarily a communication space for White supremacists, White supremacist
forum like Stormfront and Gab have also been used to collect text data 
for hate speech detection \cite{de2018hate} and study echo chambers \cite{zannettou2018gab}.
The availability of these datasets also have led to create a service
for online content moderation \cite{rieder2021fabrics}, called Perspective API.
Perspective API \footnote{\url{https://www.perspectiveapi.com/}} is a service 
hosted by Google that provides a degree of toxicity of the text input. 
Many research on incivil behavior in online space have used Perspective API 
to quantify the toxicity of the text \cite{rajadesingan2021political,chong2022understanding}.

\subsection{Problems of Hate Speech Datasets} \label{sec:problems_hate_speech_datasets}

Despite advances in NLP have made a great progress in hate speech detection,
there are still problems in hate speech detection. 
\citet{vidgen2019challenges} summarized the challenges of hate speech detection
in three categories: (1) the lack of a clear agreement on the type of hate speech (e.g., 
individual-directed abuse, identity-directed abuse, and concept-directed abuse),
(2) the lack of a clear definition of terminology in use (e.g., the intention of the speaker,
the effect of abuse, and the sensibilities of the audience) and (3) the lack of a clear
context of the text. \citeauthor{vidgen2019challenges} brings the example of the term
\textit{n-word} has an almost opposite meaning if spoken by a White compared to a Black person.

\subsection{Thomas Dixon Jr's Trilogy} \label{sec:thomas_dixon}
The full text of \textit{``The Leopard's Spots''}, \textit{``The Clansman''}, and \textit{``The Traitor''} 
are available in HathiTrust Digital Library \footnote{\url{https://babel.hathitrust.org}}. 
The text was cleaned by removing the HathiTrust terms of use and copyright 
information at the beginning and the end of the text.
Once the text is cleaned, we used BookNLP \cite{bamman2014bayesian} 
to extract the speech of the characters in the novel.
The extracted quotes are used as a training data for the hate speech detection model.

\subsection{Human Annotation} \label{sec:human_annotation}

Two authors of this study annotated the quotes extracted from the novels.
The annotation followed the definition of datasets used in this study (see section \ref{sec:datasets}).

Although the first African American immigrants did not prefer \textit{n-word} 
to refer to themselves \cite{bennett1969s}, the \textit{n-word} remained
the standard term for African Americans until the 20th century.
African American leaders like Booker T. Washington and W.E.B. Du Bois used the word 
\textit{n-word} in their speeches and writings \cite{smith1992changing}.
As the \textit{n-word} becomes a slur when speakers intentionally
use the word to degrade African Americans \cite{kennedy1999can}, 
\textit{n-word} written in the 20th century literature may not
necessarily be a derogatory term.
Culturally accepted terms change over time. Therefore, we adopted the language culture 
of the 20th century and regarded the \textit{n-word},
\textit{colored people} and/or \textit{black} not as a derogatory term in annotation.
When the \textit{n-word} is used without any word that conveys derogatory meaning,
we annotated non-hateful. That being said, whether the quote is hateful or not
was determined by the context of the quote, not by the existence of the \textit{n-word}.
Instead, we annotated the quotes that convey White supremacist ideology such as
upholding slavery and Black inferiority (such as opposing Black suffrage) as hateful.
However, general statements of restoring Confederacy and Southern pride were
not annotated as hateful.

\citet{touvron2023llama} released Llama with publicly available datasets with
open-source code, compared to GPT-3 \cite{brown2020language} which is a proprietary model.
We used Llama 2 inference model with 13B parameters implemented in C++ \footnote{\url{https://github.com/ggerganov/llama.cpp}}.
In the fine-tuning process, we used \textit{n=64} to predict 64 tokens to predict when generating text (default=128)

the parameters are set to default. We used the speech of the characters in the novel as a prompt
for the Llama 2. The generated text is evaluated with Perspective API.
